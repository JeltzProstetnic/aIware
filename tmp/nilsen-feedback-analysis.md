### 2.2 Prediction 6: Split-Brain Holographic Degradation — Wada Challenge

**André's caveats** (which he acknowledges himself):
1. Split-brain patients often have pre-existing cognitive challenges (usually epilepsy patients)
2. Residual connections remain in partial callosotomy
These are methodological complications, not theoretical refutations. The paper already addresses the graded degradation proportional to disconnection extent.

**André's core challenge — the Wada procedure**:

André's argument appears to be: Under FMT's holographic storage model, if each hemisphere retains a degraded but functionally complete copy, then temporarily anesthetizing one hemisphere (Wada) should not dramatically affect cognition — the other hemisphere should have its own degraded-but-complete copy. But Wada demonstrably DOES affect cognition: dominant hemisphere injection causes aphasia, memory deficits, etc.

**This is an important challenge but it is based on a misreading of Prediction 6.** Here is why:

#### What FMT Actually Predicts About Split-Brain vs. Wada

**Split-brain (callosotomy)** permanently severs the corpus callosum. After recovery and neural reorganization, each hemisphere independently maintains its own four-model architecture. The prediction is about the *stable end-state after reorganization* — not about what happens during acute disconnection.

**The Wada procedure** temporarily anesthetizes one hemisphere via barbiturate injection. This is fundamentally different in several ways:

1. **No time for reorganization.** The Wada effect lasts minutes. There is no opportunity for the remaining hemisphere to compensate, reorganize, or build independent model architecture. Split-brain patients have months to years of neural reorganization. FMT's holographic degradation prediction is about the stable reorganized state, not about acute loss.

2. **It doesn't just disconnect — it destroys one copy.** Callosotomy severs the *connection* between two functioning hemispheres. Wada *silences* one hemisphere entirely. Under holographic storage, you'd expect information to be distributed across both hemispheres, with each hemisphere contributing to the overall resolution. Acutely silencing half the substrate destroys half the computational capacity — including half of each model's resolution. This is like cutting a hologram in half: you still get the full image, but at lower resolution. The Wada procedure is more like covering half the hologram with an opaque sheet — the covered portion contributes nothing.

3. **Consciousness IS preserved during Wada.** This is the crucial point André may be overlooking. Wada himself noted that "actual loss of consciousness was never seen." The patient remains conscious, awake, and responsive. What changes are *specific cognitive functions* — language (if dominant hemisphere is injected), memory encoding, motor control of the contralateral side. But the person doesn't lose consciousness. This is actually *consistent* with FMT: the remaining hemisphere maintains sufficient four-model architecture for consciousness, but at reduced capacity.

4. **The specific cognitive losses match FMT predictions.** Language loss after dominant hemisphere injection = degradation of that hemisphere's IWM/EWM language processing contribution. Contralateral hemiplegia = loss of motor control mediated by the silenced hemisphere. Memory effects = degradation of IWM storage capacity. These are all examples of *graded degradation* — the very thing FMT predicts for split-brain, now observed acutely.

5. **Bilateral EEG changes during unilateral injection.** Research shows large bilateral changes in power spectral density and functional connectivity during Wada, despite behavioral changes appearing mainly on the injected side. This "cross-state unreceptiveness" via the corpus callosum means the remaining hemisphere is affected too — the acute situation is NOT equivalent to having one hemisphere in isolation.

6. **Anosognosia during Wada — SUPPORTING evidence for FMT.** After right hemisphere injection, patients frequently show anosognosia for their hemiplegia — they are unaware of their motor deficit. This is exactly what FMT's permeability mechanism predicts: the ISM's body-schema information (which is now missing the right hemisphere's contribution) cannot properly reach the EWM. This is a local permeability block — the same mechanism that explains clinical anosognosia.

#### Summary: The Wada Challenge

**Verdict: André's challenge is substantive but does not refute FMT.** It requires a careful response distinguishing:
- Acute vs. chronic disconnection (Wada vs. callosotomy)
- Silencing vs. disconnecting (destroying one copy vs. separating two copies)
- Consciousness (preserved in Wada) vs. specific cognitive functions (degraded in Wada)

**The Wada data is actually COMPATIBLE with FMT** and in some ways supportive (preserved consciousness, graded degradation, anosognosia during right injection).

**Suggested response**: "You raise an excellent point about the Wada procedure. FMT's Prediction 6 is about the stable end-state after callosotomy — where each hemisphere has had time to reorganize. The Wada test creates a fundamentally different situation: acute silencing without reorganization time. Under FMT, you'd expect exactly what Wada shows — preserved consciousness (the remaining hemisphere maintains its four-model architecture) but degraded specific functions (half the computational substrate is offline). The interesting detail is that Wada also produces anosognosia after right hemisphere injection — which FMT predicts through the same permeability mechanism that accounts for clinical anosognosia."

### 2.4 The Core Challenge: Does "Consciousness" Add Anything Beyond "Cognition"?

**André's challenge in full**: "Let's say you drop the word consciousness, and replace it with cognition or some other term. Does your theory still hold? Or to put it differently, does the introduction of 'consciousness' lead to something NEW? Most (if not all) theories of consciousness do not really require the term consciousness in them, it is just a sexy interpretive construct to put into an account of some behavior or neuroimaging results."

**This is the deepest and most important challenge in the email.** It's a variant of the deflationary objection — the claim that consciousness theories are really just cognitive theories with "consciousness" sprinkled on top.

**FMT's answer — why it IS about consciousness, not just cognition:**

1. **The real/virtual split is specifically about phenomenality, not processing.** FMT doesn't just say "the brain has four types of processing" — it says there is an ontological distinction between substrate-level processing (IWM/ISM — no experience) and simulation-level processing (EWM/ESM — constitutive of experience). Replace "consciousness" with "cognition" and this distinction evaporates. Cognition doesn't require a real/virtual split; consciousness does.

2. **Virtual qualia are a consciousness-specific claim.** The theory's central philosophical move — that qualia are properties of the computational level, like a spreadsheet cell's value is a property of the computational level — is specifically about phenomenal experience. "Cognition" doesn't need qualia. The Hard Problem is a consciousness problem, not a cognition problem. FMT's dissolution of the Hard Problem through the level-confusion argument is meaningless if you're just talking about cognition.

3. **Self-referential closure explains WHY this computation has experience.** A weather simulation has cognition (in a loose sense — it processes information, generates predictions). It doesn't have consciousness. FMT specifies what's different: the system's model includes a model of itself (ESM). This self-referential closure is why there's "something it is like" to be this system. Remove "consciousness" and you lose the explanandum entirely.

4. **The Meta-Problem is consciousness-specific.** FMT explains why we intuit that consciousness is mysterious — the ESM cannot access its own substrate, creating the structural illusion of a gap. This is specifically about phenomenal consciousness, not about cognitive processing in general.

5. **Specific predictions depend on the consciousness framing.** Prediction 3 (ego dissolution content is controllable) is about what happens to the subjective sense of identity — not about cognitive processing. Prediction 4 (psychedelics and anosognosia) connects phenomenal awareness (or lack thereof) to a permeability mechanism. Prediction 9 (DID alters) is about distinct experiential configurations, not just different cognitive profiles.

**The comparison to predictive coding is instructive here.** Predictive coding IS primarily a cognitive theory — it explains how the brain processes information. Andy Clark and Karl Friston would agree that PP is about cognition first. Seth's "controlled hallucination" framework adds phenomenality but explicitly punts on the Hard Problem. FMT doesn't punt — it offers a specific dissolution.

**Suggested response**: "This is the right question to ask. Here's my answer: replace 'consciousness' with 'cognition' and the theory's central claims become either trivially true or incoherent. The real/virtual split is specifically about why some processing has phenomenal character and other processing doesn't. Cognition doesn't need that distinction. The Hard Problem is about consciousness, not cognition — and FMT dissolves it through a specific level-confusion argument that makes no sense if we're just talking about information processing. The predictions about ego dissolution content, anosognosia, and DID alters are about subjective experience, not about cognitive performance scores. So no — 'consciousness' is doing real work in the theory, not serving as decoration."

### 2.5 How FMT Differs from Inner Screen, Predictive Coding, and World Model Theories

**André's question**: "I do see similarities with the inner screen model, predictive coding, and other world model theories. How does yours differ from these? They all posit that it is this internal model that gives rise or IS subjective experience, right?"

This is closely related to the cognition/consciousness question but deserves a specific answer.

#### FMT vs. Inner Screen Model

The "inner screen" model (Williford, Bennequin, Friston, & Rudrauf, 2018; Rudrauf et al., 2023) applies the free energy principle directly to phenomenal experience, positing "inner screens" on which experience is rendered. Similarities with FMT: both treat experience as a generated construct. **Key difference**: The inner screen model is built on the FEP mathematical framework and inherits its strengths and weaknesses. FMT provides a specific architectural taxonomy (four models, two axes) that the inner screen model lacks. FMT also provides the criticality requirement as a necessary condition — the inner screen model has no equivalent mechanism for specifying when/why screens "light up." Most importantly, FMT's level-confusion dissolution of the Hard Problem is absent from the inner screen approach.

#### FMT vs. Predictive Processing (PP)

PP (Friston, Clark, Seth) explains perception and action as inference under generative models. Seth's "controlled hallucination" is the closest PP variant to FMT. Similarities: both treat perception as model-generated, both emphasize prediction and internal simulation. **Key differences**:

| Dimension | FMT | Predictive Processing |
|-----------|-----|----------------------|
| Hard Problem | Dissolved via level confusion | Explicitly silent (Seth, 2021) |
| Architecture | Four specific models (2×2 taxonomy) | No architectural specification |
| Self-model | ESM is specific, redirectable, forkable | Self-model emerges from hierarchical inference but not architecturally specified |
| Criticality | Required for consciousness | Not a feature |
| Binding | Emergent from critical dynamics | Not addressed |
| Ego dissolution | Specific mechanism (ESM redirection) | "Breakdown of self-related priors" (vaguer) |
| Qualia | Real at computational level (virtual qualia) | Either silent or deflationary |
| Substrate independence | Explicit (four models at criticality) | Implicit (functional) |

The paper's Section 7.2 already addresses this: "The Four-Model Theory agrees that prediction is central but adds the four-model architecture, criticality requirement, and real/virtual distinction that PP lacks."

#### FMT vs. World Model Theories (generally)

"World model" is a broad category. Any theory that posits an internal model of the world is a "world model theory." FMT is one, but so is GNW (implicitly), PP, and most computational approaches. **What FMT adds beyond generic world-modeling:**

1. **Self-model as constitutive, not optional.** Many world model theories treat self-modeling as one feature among many. FMT treats it as the defining feature — consciousness IS self-simulation, specifically the ESM.
2. **The implicit/explicit distinction.** Most world model theories don't distinguish between models that are experienced and models that aren't. FMT's core is this distinction: IWM/ISM = dark, EWM/ESM = lit.
3. **Specific phenomenological predictions.** Generic world model theories don't predict ego dissolution content control, psychedelic/anosognosia connections, or DID alter signatures. FMT does.

**Suggested response**: "The similarity is real — FMT is a world-model theory, and it shares the basic intuition with predictive coding that the brain generates internal models. Where it differs: (1) PP is explicitly silent on the Hard Problem; FMT dissolves it through a specific level-confusion argument. (2) PP provides no architectural specification — 'hierarchical predictive model' is not a blueprint. FMT specifies four models along two axes, with specific properties (redirectability, forkability, clonability). (3) FMT's criticality requirement specifies when and why processing becomes conscious — PP has no equivalent threshold. (4) FMT generates specific predictions that PP doesn't: ego dissolution content tracking, psychedelic/anosognosia connection, DID alter signatures."

### 2.6 Does FMT Actually Avoid the Hard Problem?

**André's challenge**: "Does that really avoid the hard problem? It is, though, an answer to the meta-problem, but it could equally be illusionist in nature."

**Analysis**: This touches the deepest philosophical question in the paper. André is asking whether the "virtual qualia" framework actually DISSOLVES the Hard Problem or merely RELOCATES it.

**FMT's position (Section 3.4)**:
- Qualia are real at the computational level
- They are constitutive properties of the self-referential simulation
- The Hard Problem arises from a category error: seeking phenomenal properties at the substrate level where they don't exist
- This is NOT illusionism: qualia are real, not illusions. What's illusory is the assumption they must be substrate properties.

**André's objection could be restated as**: "You say qualia exist at the computational level. But WHY does computation at this level have phenomenal character? You've moved the bump under the carpet — instead of asking why neurons produce experience, you're now asking why self-referential computation produces experience."

**Fair response options**:

1. **The self-referential closure argument.** FMT's answer to "why this computation" is self-referential closure: the system models its own modeling. This collapses the inside/outside distinction. A weather simulation has an outside from which it can be fully described; a self-referential simulation at criticality has no such outside — the computation IS its own observer. This is a *principled* answer, not a deferral.

2. **The illusionism distinction matters.** Illusionism (Frankish, 2016) says there's nothing it's like — we just think there is. FMT says there IS something it's like, and it exists at the computational level. These are different claims. André may be conflating them.

3. **Honest limitation.** No theory fully explains why there is "something it is like" without some residual brute fact. FMT claims the brute fact is at the level distinction — computational processes have properties that substrate processes don't. This is less mysterious than the traditional Hard Problem because it parallels a familiar and uncontroversial phenomenon (spreadsheet values are real properties of computations that don't exist at the transistor level). But it IS still a philosophical commitment, not a proof.

**Suggested response**: "It depends what counts as 'avoiding' the Hard Problem. FMT claims to dissolve it by showing it rests on a category error — seeking phenomenal properties at the wrong level. The theory isn't illusionist: it claims qualia are real properties of the computational level, not illusions. The key argument is self-referential closure — self-modeling systems collapse the inside/outside distinction that makes the Hard Problem seem intractable. I won't claim this is a proof — no theory achieves that. But I'd argue the explanatory burden is lighter: 'why does self-referential computation have phenomenal character?' is a less mysterious question than 'why do neurons produce experience?', because we already accept that computations have properties their substrates don't."

## 3. Wada Procedure Research Summary

### What the Wada Test Is

The Wada test (intracarotid sodium amobarbital procedure, ISAP) involves injecting a fast-acting barbiturate (sodium amobarbital, 75-200mg) into one internal carotid artery, temporarily anesthetizing the ipsilateral hemisphere. Developed by Juhn Wada in 1949, originally to reduce cognitive side effects of bilateral ECT. Now used primarily for presurgical planning in epilepsy surgery.

### Cognitive Effects Observed

- **Language**: Dominant hemisphere injection → transient aphasia (minutes). Singing may be preserved (different neural substrate).
- **Motor**: Contralateral hemiplegia (paralysis of opposite-side limbs)
- **Memory**: Memory encoding in the anesthetized hemisphere is impaired; the contralateral hemisphere's memory capacity is tested
- **Personality**: Disinhibition common; drastic personality changes rare
- **Consciousness**: PRESERVED. Wada himself noted "actual loss of consciousness was never seen." Patients remain awake, responsive, and aware — though sometimes mildly confused.
- **Anosognosia**: After right hemisphere injection, patients frequently fail to recall their hemiplegia. This is a transient, experimentally induced anosognosia.

### Key Research Findings Relevant to FMT

1. **Consciousness persists during unilateral anesthesia** — consistent with FMT's claim that each hemisphere maintains sufficient architecture for consciousness.

2. **Bilateral EEG changes despite unilateral injection** (2020 study) — power spectral density and functional connectivity change across BOTH hemispheres, explained by corpus callosum conduction. This means Wada isn't a clean "one hemisphere off, one hemisphere on" scenario.

3. **Dominant hemisphere injection produces more confusion** — consistent with FMT's language-processing being more lateralized than general consciousness.

4. **Bilateral hippocampal dysfunction → severe consciousness impairment** (case report) — when both hippocampi are offline (one permanently lesioned, one acutely anesthetized), consciousness is severely compromised. This supports FMT's claim that sufficient substrate must be available.

5. **Wada-induced anosognosia** (1992, 1997 studies) — right hemisphere injection → unawareness of hemiplegia. This directly supports FMT's permeability mechanism.

### FMT Implications

FMT's Prediction 6 is about callosotomy (permanent disconnection with reorganization time), not about acute hemisphere silencing. The Wada test challenges a straw-man version of Prediction 6 that FMT doesn't actually make. However, the response should explicitly clarify this distinction, because readers could make the same misreading André made.

**Recommendation**: Consider adding a brief Wada discussion to the paper's Section 6.4 (Split-Brain) or Section 8.6 (Prediction 6), proactively distinguishing acute silencing from chronic disconnection.

## 4. How FMT Differentiates from Predictive Coding and World Model Theories

### The Differentiation Problem

André's concern is legitimate: at surface level, FMT looks like another world-model theory. Many theories claim "the brain generates internal models." The question is whether FMT adds genuine explanatory value.

### Five Points of Genuine Differentiation

**1. The Hard Problem dissolution (PP punts; FMT engages)**
Seth (2021) explicitly says PP addresses the "real problem" of consciousness — explaining structure and contents — but does not address the Hard Problem as Chalmers defines it. FMT directly addresses the Hard Problem through the level-confusion argument and virtual qualia framework. This isn't a trivial addition — it's the central question of consciousness science.

**2. Architectural specificity (PP is vague; FMT is concrete)**
PP says "hierarchical generative model" but doesn't specify what the model's architecture must look like. FMT specifies: four model kinds along two axes (scope × mode), with specific properties (implicit = substrate-level, explicit = simulation-level, each with distinct characteristics — storability, redirectability, forkability).

**3. The criticality requirement (PP has no threshold)**
FMT specifies a necessary condition for consciousness: the substrate must operate at criticality (Wolfram Class 4). PP has no equivalent — it has no mechanism to explain why some predictive processing is conscious and some isn't (which is a known weakness; see Hohwy 2013 on the "prediction error puzzle").

**4. Specific cross-domain predictions (PP generates none comparable)**
FMT's Predictions 3, 4, and 9 are highly specific and uniquely generated:
- Ego dissolution content tracking (Pred 3)
- Psychedelic/anosognosia connection (Pred 4)
- DID alter neural signatures (Pred 9)
PP might retrospectively accommodate these but doesn't predict them.

**5. The real/virtual ontological distinction (PP is epistemological)**
PP is primarily an epistemological framework — it describes how the brain processes information. FMT makes an ontological claim: there is a genuine difference in kind between substrate-level and simulation-level processes. The virtual side HAS phenomenal properties; the real side doesn't. This is a stronger claim than PP makes.

### What FMT Shares with These Theories (and should acknowledge)

- Internal modeling as central to brain function (shared with PP, world model theories)
- Prediction and inference as core processing (shared with PP)
- Self-modeling as important for consciousness (shared with AST, SMT)
- Experience as generated/constructed, not direct (shared with PP, inner screen)

**FMT is a synthesis that adds:** specific architecture + criticality threshold + ontological level distinction + novel cross-domain predictions. It is not merely a repackaging — but the response should frankly acknowledge the shared foundations.
