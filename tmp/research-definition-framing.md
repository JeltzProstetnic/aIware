**Date**: 2026-03-16
**Context**: The Four-Model Theory (FMT) was desk-rejected twice by *Neuroscience of Consciousness* (editor: Thomas Andrillon). The second rejection's key complaint: "the definition departs from the standard focus on subjective experience and centers on the self." This report investigates whether that criticism is justified, maps the full attack surface, and recommends specific paper edits.

## 1. How Major Theories Define Consciousness

There is no single "standard definition" of consciousness. The major theories define their target phenomenon differently — and most do NOT center on "subjective experience" as their primary explanandum. Here is what each theory actually says:

### 1.1 Integrated Information Theory (IIT) — Tononi, Koch

**Definition**: Consciousness IS integrated information (Phi). "Consciousness is identical to a certain kind of information, the realization of which requires physical, not merely functional, integration" (Tononi, 2004). IIT 4.0 (Albantakis et al., 2023) defines consciousness in terms of intrinsic causal power: a system is conscious to the degree it has irreducible cause-effect power over itself.

**What it centers on**: Integrated information / intrinsic causal structure. NOT subjective experience directly — IIT *identifies* subjective experience with Phi, which is a mathematical-structural property.

**Key point**: IIT's definition departs radically from common usage. It implies that a photodiode has micro-consciousness (panpsychism), that grid systems might have more Phi than brains, and over 100 researchers have called it unfalsifiable pseudoscience. Yet no editor desk-rejects IIT papers for "departing from the standard definition."

### 1.2 Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) — Dehaene, Changeux; Global Workspace Theory (GWT) — Baars

**Definition**: Consciousness is global broadcasting / access. A mental state is conscious when it is amplified by a non-linear "ignition" in prefrontal-parietal networks and broadcast to all specialized processors, making it globally available for report, reasoning, and action.

**What it centers on**: Access consciousness — availability for use, reportability. Baars's original metaphor: consciousness is like a spotlight on a theater stage, making information available to the audience (specialized processors).

**Key point**: GNW is *explicitly* a theory of access consciousness, not phenomenal consciousness. Dehaene acknowledges this. Block (1995, 2007) argued that access consciousness and phenomenal consciousness can come apart. GNW defines consciousness in terms of a mechanism (broadcasting), not in terms of subjective experience.

### 1.3 Higher-Order Theories (HOT) — Rosenthal, Lau, Brown

**Definition**: A mental state is conscious when it is the target of a higher-order representation — a thought about that state. "A conscious mental state M is a state that is actually causing an activated thought (generally a non-conscious one) that one has M" (Rosenthal).

**What it centers on**: Self-directed representation. A state is conscious not by virtue of what it's like, but by virtue of being represented by a higher-order state. This is explicitly a self-referential / meta-representational definition.

**Key point**: HOT is structurally similar to FMT's self-simulation move. Both say: consciousness requires the system to represent its own states. HOT defines consciousness through self-directed representation, not through subjective experience directly. Subjective experience is what results from higher-order representation, not what defines it.

### 1.4 Predictive Processing / Active Inference — Friston, Seth, Clark

**Definition**: Seth defines consciousness as "any kind of subjective experience whatsoever" but then immediately operationalizes "the real problem" as: how do mechanisms in the brain and body explain, predict, and control *properties* of consciousness? His actual theoretical machinery centers on controlled hallucination, interoceptive inference, and the brain as a prediction machine.

**What it centers on**: Prediction error minimization, generative models, and — crucially — the self-model. Seth's "beast machine" theory explicitly centers selfhood: "we experience the world and the self — with, through, and because of our living bodies." Interoceptive inference generates the sense of embodied selfhood. Self-modeling is the core mechanism.

**Key point**: Seth's work is arguably the closest to FMT in centering self-modeling as the mechanism for consciousness. His definition starts with "subjective experience" but his explanatory work is entirely about self-modeling, prediction, and interoception. The mechanism IS self-modeling. If centering on the self is non-standard, then Seth's entire research program is non-standard.

### 1.5 Attention Schema Theory (AST) — Graziano

**Definition**: Subjective awareness IS the brain's internal model of attention. "The brain constructs a simplified model of the process of attention... subjective experience is a simplified construct that describes the act of attending to something."

**What it centers on**: Self-modeling of attention. AST is explicitly deflationary: there is no "hard" phenomenality beyond what the attention schema represents. Consciousness is the brain's model of its own attention — a self-model.

**Key point**: AST defines consciousness as a specific kind of self-model. It is squarely in the self-modeling tradition. No editor desk-rejects AST papers for "centering on the self rather than subjective experience."

### 1.6 Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity (SMT) — Metzinger

**Definition**: Consciousness arises from a transparent phenomenal self-model (PSM). "No such things as selves exist in the world: nobody ever had or was a self. All that exists are phenomenal selves, as they appear in conscious experience... the content of a 'transparent self-model'" (Metzinger, 2003).

**What it centers on**: Self-modeling. Metzinger's entire theory is that consciousness IS self-modeling. The phenomenal self-model is the constitutive mechanism. This is published in MIT Press, cited thousands of times, and is one of the most influential theories of consciousness.

**Key point**: SMT is FMT's closest theoretical ancestor (FMT itself acknowledges this). If centering consciousness on self-modeling is non-standard, then Metzinger's 700-page MIT Press monograph and everything built on it is non-standard.

### 1.7 Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT) — Lamme

**Definition**: Consciousness is recurrent (feedback) processing in sensory cortex. Processing becomes conscious when feed-forward signals are supplemented by recurrent connections creating loops of activity.

**What it centers on**: A neural mechanism (recurrent processing), not subjective experience. Lamme argues that recurrent processing in posterior cortex is sufficient for phenomenal consciousness, even without reportability.

**Key point**: Another theory that defines consciousness by its mechanism, not by "what it's like."

### Summary Table

| Theory | Centers definition on | Subjective experience is... |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| IIT | Integrated information (Phi) | Identical to Phi (by axiom) |
| GNW/GWT | Global broadcasting / access | What broadcasting produces |
| HOT | Higher-order self-representation | What higher-order states produce |
| PP/Seth | Prediction + self-modeling | What controlled hallucination produces |
| AST | Self-model of attention | What the attention schema represents |
| SMT | Phenomenal self-model | What transparent self-modeling produces |
| RPT | Recurrent processing | What recurrent dynamics produce |
| **FMT** | **Self-simulation** | **What the virtual models produce** |

**Conclusion**: Of the seven major theories, ZERO define consciousness primarily as "subjective experience." Every theory defines consciousness in terms of a mechanism, structure, or process — and then explains subjective experience as what that mechanism produces. FMT does exactly the same thing.

## 2. Is "Subjective Experience" Really THE Standard Definition?

### 2.1 The Philosophical Landscape

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP) entry on consciousness identifies at least six distinct concepts that "consciousness" can refer to:

1. **Creature consciousness** — the organism is awake/responsive
2. **State consciousness** — a mental state has subjective character
3. **Phenomenal consciousness** — "what it is like" (Nagel, 1974)
4. **Access consciousness** — availability for reasoning and report (Block, 1995)
5. **Self-consciousness** — awareness of oneself
6. **Monitoring consciousness** — meta-cognitive awareness

The SEP explicitly notes: there is no agreed-upon definition. The field has "deep confusion and internal division" because researchers lack "a sufficiently well-specified use of the term to agree that they are investigating the same thing."

### 2.2 The Phenomenal vs. Access Split

Ned Block's (1995) distinction between phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness: "what it is like") and access consciousness (A-consciousness: availability for use) remains one of the most debated issues in the field. Major theories take different sides:

- **GNW** is explicitly a theory of access consciousness
- **IIT** claims to address phenomenal consciousness but through mathematical structure, not "what it's like"
- **HOT** explains phenomenal consciousness through meta-representation
- **RPT** claims phenomenal consciousness is in sensory cortex; access is separate
- **PP/Seth** proposes the "real problem" as an alternative to the "hard problem" — explicitly sidestepping the phenomenal/access debate

Block's "overflow" argument — that phenomenal consciousness overflows access — means that even within the field, there is deep disagreement about whether the "what it's like" notion can be cleanly separated from functional properties.

### 2.3 Is Andrillon Applying a Narrow Framing?

**Yes.** Andrillon's criticism — that FMT "departs from the standard focus on subjective experience" — implicitly treats Nagel/Chalmers-style phenomenal consciousness as THE standard. But:

1. **There is no standard.** The SEP, IEP, and every major review paper explicitly say so.
2. **Most neuroscientific theories do not center on phenomenal consciousness.** GNW centers on access, PP centers on prediction/self-modeling, AST centers on attention self-models, HOT centers on meta-representation.
3. **The "standard focus on subjective experience" is a philosopher's framing, not a neuroscientist's.** Experimental consciousness researchers typically operationalize consciousness as reportability (access), neural correlates, or behavioral markers — not "what it's like."
4. **The Chalmers/Nagel framing is not universally accepted even among philosophers.** Dennett, Frankish, and the entire illusionist tradition reject it. Seth explicitly proposes the "real problem" as an alternative.

Andrillon's criticism reveals a specific editorial preference, not a scientific standard. It is the equivalent of an editor rejecting GNW for "only addressing access consciousness, not phenomenal consciousness" — a criticism that could be leveled at GNW but would be recognized as applying a specific philosophical commitment, not an objective standard.

### 2.4 The Self-Modeling Tradition: Not Fringe, But Central

There is a substantial, respected tradition in consciousness studies that centers consciousness on self-modeling:

| Author(s) | Work | Year | Key claim |
|-----------|------|------|-----------|
| William James | *Principles of Psychology* | 1890 | Distinguished "I" (subjective agent) from "Me" (objective self-concept) |
| John Locke | *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* | 1690 | Self-consciousness is partly definitive of personhood |
| Thomas Metzinger | *Being No One* (MIT Press) | 2003 | Consciousness = transparent phenomenal self-model |
| Antonio Damasio | *The Feeling of What Happens*, *Self Comes to Mind* | 1999, 2010 | Consciousness emerges through proto-self → core self → autobiographical self |
| Douglas Hofstadter | *I Am a Strange Loop* | 2007 | Consciousness = self-referential strange loop |
| Michael Graziano | *Consciousness and the Social Brain* | 2013 | Consciousness = self-model of attention |
| Anil Seth | *Being You* | 2021 | Self-modeling via interoceptive inference |
| Kriegel & Williford | *Self-Representational Approaches to Consciousness* | 2006 | Consciousness always involves self-representation |
| Karl Friston | Active inference framework | 2010+ | Selfhood inherent in inference about control |

This is not a fringe tradition. It includes two MIT Press monographs, multiple Cambridge/Oxford publications, a Princeton neuroscience lab, and the Free Energy Principle — arguably the most ambitious unifying framework in neuroscience. FMT sits squarely within this tradition.

## 3. Is FMT's Self-Simulation Framing Actually Non-Standard?

### 3.1 Comparison with Metzinger's Self-Model Theory

FMT and SMT share the core architectural claim: consciousness is constituted by self-modeling. Key parallels:

| Feature | SMT (Metzinger) | FMT (Gruber) |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| Core claim | Consciousness = phenomenal self-model | Consciousness = ongoing self-simulation |
| Self as process | Yes — no substantial self, only a process | Yes — consciousness is a process, not a property |
| Transparency | Self-model is "transparent" (not experienced as a model) | ESM narrativizes continuity; substrate is structurally inaccessible |
| No homunculus | Explicitly denies inner observer | Explicitly denies inner observer |
| Substrate independence | Implied | Explicit (with criticality requirement) |

FMT extends SMT by: (a) adding the four-model taxonomy (implicit/explicit x world/self), (b) adding the criticality requirement, (c) providing the virtual qualia framework to dissolve the Hard Problem, and (d) generating specific empirical predictions. FMT is an extension of SMT, not a departure from the field.

### 3.2 Comparison with Damasio's Framework

Damasio's three-level self (proto-self, core self, autobiographical self) parallels FMT's graduated consciousness hierarchy (basic, simply extended, doubly extended, triply extended). Both ground consciousness in increasingly sophisticated self-modeling.

| Feature | Damasio | FMT |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Core mechanism | Self-representing organism | Self-simulating system |
| Graduated levels | Proto-self → core self → autobiographical self | Basic → simply → doubly → triply extended |
| Emotion as foundational | Feelings arise from body-state modeling | ESM registers interoceptive states |
| Embodiment | Central | Compatible (substrate independence allows but doesn't require embodiment) |

Damasio explicitly centers consciousness on the self: "consciousness, at its simplest, is the feeling of what happens when a neural pattern of self is modified by interaction with a neural pattern of an object." This is a definition that centers on the self and its modeling, not on "subjective experience" in the abstract.

### 3.3 Comparison with Predictive Processing's Self-Modeling

Seth's beast machine theory proposes that:
- Perceptual experience = controlled hallucination (top-down prediction)
- Emotional experience = interoceptive inference
- Selfhood = a special kind of controlled hallucination
- The self is "another perception, another controlled hallucination, though of a very special kind"

This is self-modeling as the constitutive mechanism of consciousness — the same structural claim as FMT.

### 3.4 Comparison with HOT's Self-Referential Structure

Higher-Order Theories define consciousness through self-directed representation. A state is conscious BECAUSE the system represents itself as being in that state. This is a self-referential definition. FMT's self-simulation is a richer version of the same core idea: consciousness requires the system to model itself.

### 3.5 Comparison with Hofstadter's Strange Loops

Hofstadter defines the "I" as "a self-referential strange loop — a symbolic self-model that the brain uses to represent and influence itself." FMT's self-referential closure (Section 3.4 of the paper) is architecturally the same idea, made more specific through the four-model taxonomy and criticality requirement.

### 3.6 Verdict: FMT Is Not Non-Standard

FMT's self-simulation framing places it within a lineage that includes: Metzinger (SMT), Damasio (proto-self/core-self), Hofstadter (strange loops), Graziano (AST), Seth (beast machine), Kriegel/Williford (self-representationalism), and the active inference tradition (Friston). This is not a fringe position. It is one of the two or three dominant approaches to consciousness in contemporary philosophy and neuroscience.

**What IS unusual about FMT is not the self-modeling claim but:**
1. The specific four-model taxonomy (2x2: implicit/explicit x world/self)
2. The virtual qualia framework (qualia as computational-level properties)
3. The criticality requirement (edge-of-chaos dynamics as prerequisite)
4. The specific claim that self-simulation dissolves (not merely addresses) the Hard Problem

These are the genuinely novel claims. The self-modeling foundation is mainstream.

## 4. How to Frame FMT's Definition to Minimize Attack Surface

### 4.1 The Core Argument: Mechanism vs. Output

The key rhetorical move is simple: **centering on the mechanism that generates subjective experience is not departing from subjective experience — it is explaining it.**

Every mature science does this:
- Biology doesn't define "life" as "the feeling of being alive" — it defines it through metabolic processes, self-replication, homeostasis
- Physics doesn't define "heat" as "the sensation of warmth" — it defines it as molecular kinetic energy
- Neuroscience doesn't define "vision" as "the subjective experience of seeing" — it defines it through retinal transduction, neural pathways, cortical processing

FMT defines consciousness through the mechanism (self-simulation) that generates the output (subjective experience). This is standard scientific practice, not a departure.

### 4.2 Bridging Language: Connecting Self-Simulation to Subjective Experience

The paper currently defines consciousness in Section 3.1 as:

> "Consciousness is the ability of an entity — biological or artificial — to create a model of itself, to relate that model to itself, and to interact with it."

This definition needs a bridge sentence that explicitly connects to the subjective experience literature. Proposed addition (immediately after the current definition):

> **Proposed bridging paragraph**: "This definition centers on the mechanism — self-simulation — rather than the output — subjective experience — for the same reason that molecular biology defines 'life' through metabolism and replication rather than through 'the feeling of being alive': centering on the mechanism is what makes the definition explanatory rather than circular. Subjective experience, on this account, is what ongoing self-simulation produces when operating at criticality (Section 3.7). The theory's central explanatory task is to show how and why self-simulation generates phenomenality (Section 3.4), not to stipulate that it does. This approach is shared by the self-modeling tradition in consciousness studies — Metzinger's Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity (Metzinger, 2003), Damasio's proto-self framework (Damasio, 1999, 2010), Seth's interoceptive inference account (Seth, 2021), and the self-representational approaches surveyed by Kriegel and Williford (2006) — all of which define consciousness through the self-modeling mechanism that constitutes it."

### 4.3 Proposed Introduction Addition

The paper's Introduction (Section 1.3) currently jumps from historical context to theory overview. A short paragraph should be added to explicitly locate FMT within the self-modeling tradition:

> **Proposed paragraph for Section 1.3 (after "neural network architecture" sentence)**: "The theory belongs to the self-modeling tradition in consciousness studies, which holds that consciousness is constituted by, rather than merely correlated with, the system's model of itself. This tradition includes Metzinger's Self-Model Theory of Subjectivity (Metzinger, 2003, 2009), which analyzed the phenomenal self-model as a transparent representational structure; Damasio's multi-level self framework (Damasio, 1999, 2010), which grounded consciousness in progressively elaborated self-representations; Hofstadter's strange-loop account (Hofstadter, 2007), which identified the self with self-referential symbolic structures; Graziano's Attention Schema Theory (Graziano, 2013), which modeled subjective awareness as the brain's schema of its own attention; and Seth's interoceptive inference framework (Seth, 2021), which cast the experienced self as a controlled hallucination grounded in body-state prediction. The Four-Model Theory extends this tradition by specifying the minimal architecture required for consciousness-constituting self-simulation (four models along two axes), adding a physical prerequisite (criticality), and developing a two-level ontology (real/virtual) that dissolves the Hard Problem."

### 4.4 Proposed Revision to Section 3.1 Core Definition

The current opening of Section 3.1 is:

> "Consciousness is the ability of an entity — biological or artificial — to create a model of itself, to relate that model to itself, and to interact with it. Consciousness is not a property the brain possesses but a process the brain performs: it runs an ongoing self-simulation."

Proposed revision that front-loads the connection to subjective experience:

> "Consciousness — the capacity for subjective experience — is constituted by a specific kind of process: ongoing self-simulation. More precisely, consciousness is the ability of an entity — biological or artificial — to create a model of itself, to relate that model to itself, and to interact with it. Consciousness is not a property the brain possesses but a process the brain performs. Subjective experience is what this process generates when the substrate operates at the edge of chaos (Section 3.7). The theory's explanatory task is to show *why* self-simulation at criticality produces phenomenality (Section 3.4), not merely to assert that it does."

This revision:
1. Opens with "subjective experience" — immediately signaling that the paper addresses the standard concern
2. Presents self-simulation as the constitutive mechanism, not as an alternative to subjective experience
3. Connects to the Hard Problem dissolution (Section 3.4) and criticality (Section 3.7)
4. Explicitly promises to explain WHY subjective experience emerges, not just to redefine it

### 5.3 Detailed Analysis of Each Objection

#### Objection A: Definition non-standard

**Is it justified?** No. As documented in Sections 1-3 of this report, centering consciousness on self-modeling is a mainstream position held by Metzinger, Damasio, Seth, Graziano, Hofstadter, Kriegel, and the active inference tradition. The criticism reveals either: (a) Andrillon's personal preference for phenomenal-consciousness-first framing, or (b) the paper's failure to explicitly locate itself within the self-modeling tradition, making it APPEAR non-standard.

**Fix**: Both (a) and (b). Add the bridging language from Section 4 above. This doesn't change what the paper says; it changes how it frames what it says. The definition remains self-simulation, but it is now explicitly connected to subjective experience and located within a recognized tradition.

**Is this gatekeeping?** Partially. Andrillon's framing ("the standard focus on subjective experience") implies a consensus that does not exist. However, the paper genuinely failed to locate itself within the self-modeling tradition — and that omission made the definition look more radical than it is. The fix is editorial, not substantive.

#### Objection B: Predictions too general

**Is it justified?** Mixed. The nine predictions span a range from very specific to quite general:

| Prediction | Specificity | Assessment |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| P1: Distinct fMRI signatures for each model | General — many theories predict distributed neural correlates | Needs sharpening or dropping |
| P2: Psychedelic content maps processing hierarchy | Moderate — dose-dependent ordered progression is specific | Keep, add experimental design |
| P3: Ego dissolution content is controllable | **Highly specific** — uniquely FMT, testable now | Keep — this is FMT's strongest prediction |
| P4: Psychedelics alleviate anosognosia | **Highly specific** — connects psychopharmacology and clinical neurology | Keep — novel, testable, connects domains |
| P5: All anesthetics converge on criticality disruption | Moderate — consistent with ConCrit, but other theories predict similar | Keep with sharper framing |
| P6: Split-brain produces holographic degradation | Moderate — specific about symmetrical degradation | Keep, add experimental specifics |
| P7 (TI-1): Artificial consciousness requires criticality + 4 models | Not currently testable | Move to implications |
| P8: Sleep architecture reflects criticality maintenance | Moderate — connects to empirical sleep research | Keep, sharpen |
| P9: DID alters have distinct neural process signatures | Moderate — testable but needs more specifics | Keep, add experimental design |
| P10 (TI-2): Building consciousness | Not currently testable | Move to implications |

**Recommendation**: Focus the predictions section on the 4-5 strongest (P2, P3, P4, P5, P8), each with a proposed experimental design. P3 (controllable ego dissolution) and P4 (psychedelics for anosognosia) are FMT's unique selling propositions — no other theory generates them.

#### Objection C: REM sleep factual error

**Is it justified?** Yes. Sensory processing during REM sleep is attenuated but not absent. There is well-documented evidence of auditory processing during REM (e.g., incorporation of external stimuli into dreams, K-complex-like responses). The paper needs a factual correction here.

**Fix**: Replace any claim of "absent" sensory input during REM with "profoundly attenuated" or "gated." Cite Nir & Tononi (2010) on thalamocortical gating, Andrillon et al. (2016 — yes, the editor himself published on this) on auditory processing during sleep, and Strauss et al. (2015) on residual sensory processing. This is a straightforward factual correction, not a theoretical revision.

#### Objection D: Needs stronger integration with existing literature

**Is it justified?** Partially. The paper has 73+ references and a detailed comparison table (Section 7), which is more than many theoretical papers. However, it does NOT explicitly locate FMT within the self-modeling tradition. It mentions Metzinger and Dennett as ancestors (Section 1.3) but doesn't systematically connect to Damasio, Seth, Graziano, Hofstadter, or Kriegel as fellow self-modeling theorists.

**Fix**: The tradition-locating paragraph proposed in Section 4.3 above. Also consider adding a brief subsection to Section 7 comparing FMT specifically to the self-modeling tradition (not just to individual theories).
