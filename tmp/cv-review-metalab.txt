2. CREDIBILITY FOR AN ACADEMIC AUDIENCE
-----------------------------------------

This is the central challenge. Here is what an academic reviewer sees:

STRENGTHS:
  - ORCID listed (signals awareness of academic norms)
  - Two papers under review at real journals (NoC and NIdP are both legitimate)
  - Preprints available (Zenodo + PsyArXiv — shows work is accessible)
  - Engineering degree + doctoral studies = real academic training
  - 2015 book establishes long-term engagement, not a dilettante

WEAKNESSES:
  a) No completed doctorate. "Dr. techn. studies (doctoral) 2009-2011" is listed
     but there is no degree conferred. Academics will notice this immediately. It
     looks like you started a PhD and didn't finish. The thesis (2016) has an ISBN
     and is listed as a publication, but the education section doesn't say "Dr. techn."
     as the degree. This creates ambiguity: did you get the doctorate or not?

     CRITICAL: If you DID complete the doctorate, list it as "Dr. techn." not
     "Dr. techn. studies (doctoral)." If you did NOT complete it, you need to be
     transparent — "doctoral studies (thesis submitted, not defended)" or similar.
     The current wording is evasive.

  b) The consciousness publications are all from 2025 (or 2015 German book).
     There is a 10-year publication gap in the consciousness domain. Academics
     will wonder: what happened between 2015 and 2025?

  c) No peer-reviewed consciousness publications yet. Both papers are "under
     review" or "submitted." The 2015 book is self-published (Lulu.com, given
     the ISBN prefix 978-1-326). Academics can look up ISBN prefixes. Self-published

  d) The non-consciousness publications (2006, 2010, 2016) are in completely
     different fields (wearable sensors, production optimization, simulation).
     They show academic competence but not consciousness expertise.

  e) No h-index, no citation metrics. This is normal for someone without published
     work, but it means the CV stands or falls on the papers under review.

VERDICT: An honest assessment — this CV is borderline for an academic audience.
The committee will see: industry professional, no completed PhD (or ambiguously
completed), no peer-reviewed publications in consciousness, two papers under
review. The 20+ years of independent research is actually a red flag to some
academics: "Why has none of this been published until now?" You should consider
addressing this proactively in the cover statement, not the CV.

4. RED FLAGS AND WEAKNESSES
-----------------------------

RED FLAG 1: THE DOCTORATE AMBIGUITY
As discussed above. This is the single biggest issue. If the doctorate is incomplete,
the CV is deceptive. If complete, the CV is poorly formatted. Fix this.

RED FLAG 2: "BORN 1978" IN THE HEADER
The summer school targets "graduate students and postdoctoral fellows." Putting
your birth year in the header immediately signals you are 47 — far outside the
typical age range. Academics reading CVs for student/postdoc programs will notice.
REMOVE the birth year. It serves no purpose and actively hurts.

RED FLAG 3: SELF-PUBLISHED BOOK
ISBN 978-1-326-65207-4 is a Lulu.com ISBN. Anyone who checks will see this.
Self-publishing is not inherently bad, but listing it alongside journal submissions
without distinguishing it risks looking like you're inflating your publication record.
Consider: keep it, but add "(self-published)" explicitly. Honesty preempts suspicion.

RED FLAG 4: THE PHOTO
Academic CVs in the UK/US do NOT include photos. This is a continental European
convention. For a UCL-based program, a photo is unusual and may trigger unconscious
bias (positive or negative). Fleming's lab is at UCL. Remove the photo.

RED FLAG 5: NO REFERENCES / RECOMMENDERS
Academic applications typically want references. If the form asks for them, you
have a problem — who would serve as an academic reference for consciousness research?
Breitenecker (doctoral supervisor) is in simulation, not consciousness. Lukowicz
(BSc supervisor) is in wearable computing. Neither validates your consciousness work.
This cannot be fixed on the CV, but be aware.

RED FLAG 6: "INDEPENDENT RESEARCH PROGRAM SINCE 2003"
This is meant to signal dedication but to an academic it reads: "I've been working
on this for 22 years and have zero peer-reviewed publications." It invites the
question: why? Consider rephrasing: "Developed 2003-2015; formalized and submitted
for peer review 2024-2025" — which tells a story of maturation rather than stagnation.

MINOR FLAG: "NINE FALSIFIABLE PREDICTIONS"
Claiming nine falsifiable predictions is bold. If the reviewers check and find them
vague or untestable, this backfires. For a CV, "generates testable predictions"
is safer than committing to a specific number. The number is better saved for the
innovation proposal where you can back it up.

MINOR FLAG: CONFERENCE SUBMISSIONS INCLUDE ONE "IN PREPARATION"
Never list work "in preparation" on a CV for an academic application. It looks
like padding. Remove the Bochum line entirely, or wait until it's submitted.

8. THE "INDEPENDENT RESEARCHER" PROBLEM
-----------------------------------------

This is the elephant in the room. The summer school is for "graduate students
and postdoctoral fellows." Matthias is neither.

HOW THE CV CURRENTLY HANDLES IT:
  - Lists "Independent Consciousness Researcher" in the header
  - Shows research activity (publications, conferences)
  - Shows academic background (degrees, doctoral studies)
  - Does NOT address the eligibility mismatch directly

HOW IT SHOULD HANDLE IT:
  a) The CV alone cannot solve this. The cover statement / statement of purpose
     MUST address it head-on. Something like: "While I am not currently in a
     postdoctoral position, my research activity — two papers under review at
     peer-reviewed journals, a 22-year independent research program, and active
     conference participation — represents an equivalent level of scholarly
     engagement."

  b) The CV should support this by MAXIMIZING research signals and MINIMIZING
     corporate signals. Every line that talks about Scrum, FDA compliance, or
     voestalpine makes the reader think "industry person" instead of "researcher."

  c) Consider listing "Ivoclar AG" as an affiliation on the form but framing it
     as: "Ivoclar AG (industry) / Independent researcher (consciousness science)"
     — making clear that you have an institutional base even if it's not academic.

  d) The V-Research and PROFACTOR roles ARE research positions. Emphasize these
     more and the Ivoclar/OMICRON roles less.

HONEST ASSESSMENT: There is a non-trivial chance the application is rejected on
eligibility grounds regardless of CV quality. The committee may simply filter for
university affiliations. The best strategy is to make the research case SO
compelling and the innovation proposal SO specific that they make an exception.
The CV is a supporting document — the innovation proposal is what will actually
get you in.
