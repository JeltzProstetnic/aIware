Dear Editors,

I am submitting "The Four-Model Theory of Consciousness: A Simulation-Based Framework Unifying the Hard Problem, Binding, and Altered States" for consideration as a Review Article in *Neuroscience of Consciousness*.

An earlier version of this manuscript was submitted as NCONSC-2026-051 and desk-rejected on March 3, 2026. Dr. Andrillon's feedback identified three specific weaknesses, all of which have been addressed in this revision:

1. **Sharper definition of "simulation."** I have added a clarification paragraph in Section 3.1 making explicit that "self-simulation" is pedagogical shorthand — the brain generates a unified narrative, not a digital twin. The substrate/computation distinction is presented as universal to all computing systems. "Simulated" has been replaced with "generated" in model definitions and Table 1 throughout.

2. **Clearer Hard Problem dissolution.** Section 3.4 has been restructured. It now opens by establishing the substrate/computation level distinction as an engineering truism, then defines qualia as constitutive properties of the computational level. The Hard Problem is framed as a level confusion: seeking phenomenal properties at the substrate level where they categorically do not exist.

3. **Self-referential closure promoted to primary argument.** The previous version buried this in an anticipated-objection paragraph. It is now the primary dissolution argument: the four-model architecture creates a closed loop where the system models its own modeling, collapsing the model/modeled distinction — which is why a weather simulation lacks experience but a self-referential system at criticality does not.

The paper presents a theory of consciousness — originally published in German in 2015 and now formalized for the English-language scientific literature — in which consciousness is constituted by real-time self-simulation across four nested models (implicit/explicit world and self models) operating on a substrate at the edge of chaos. The theory's central claim is that qualia are virtual: properties of the simulation, not the substrate. This dissolves the Hard Problem by revealing a category error, without recourse to panpsychism or strong emergence.

I believe this paper is timely and relevant to your readership for three reasons:

**1. The criticality convergence.** The theory's criticality requirement was derived from Wolfram's computational framework in 2015 — independently of, though not prior to, the empirical criticality program — and converges with the empirical literature consolidated through Hengen and Shew's meta-analysis of 140 datasets (Neuron, 2025) and Algom and Shriki's ConCrit framework (Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, 2026). This convergence — theoretical prediction confirmed by large-scale empirical synthesis — is, I believe, noteworthy.

**2. Unique testable predictions.** The theory generates nine novel predictions, several of which no competing theory can produce. Most distinctive: that the content of psychedelic ego dissolution is controllable via sensory input (the redirectable Explicit Self Model mechanism), and that psychedelics should alleviate anosognosia (connecting psychopharmacology and clinical neurology through variable implicit-explicit permeability). These predictions are experimentally tractable with current methods.

**3. Systematic comparison.** The paper evaluates the theory against all eight core requirements a complete theory of consciousness must meet (Hard Problem, Explanatory Gap, Boundary, Structure, Binding, Combination, Causal Role, Meta-Problem) and provides a systematic comparison with IIT, GNW, HOT, PP, AST, and RPT — including the COGITATE results and emerging frameworks from 2024-2026.

The manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration by another journal. The theory was originally published in a German-language book (Gruber, 2015); this paper is the first formal presentation in the scientific literature. The updated preprint (v3) is available at https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.18861613.
