Dear Prof. Carhart-Harris,

I am writing with a specific, testable prediction about psychedelic ego dissolution that I believe your lab is uniquely positioned to test.

The prediction: during ego dissolution, the *content* of the altered identity experience (what the subject "becomes") tracks the dominant sensory input. By controlling the sensory environment during ego dissolution, the identity content can be predicted and directed.

This prediction derives from a theory of consciousness I developed in 2015 (published in German as *Die Emergenz des Bewusstseins*) and have now written up as a formal scientific paper in English. The Four-Model Theory models the conscious self as a real-time simulation (the "Explicit Self Model" or ESM). During ego dissolution, the ESM does not cease to exist; it loses its normal self-referential input and latches onto whatever sensory input dominates. This is dramatically confirmed by salvia divinorum phenomenology, where users reliably report "becoming" objects in their immediate environment — furniture, walls, characters from television playing in the room.

The experimental design is straightforward: administer ego-dissolution-inducing doses under controlled conditions, vary the dominant sensory input across conditions, and measure correspondence between input and reported identity content. No competing theory of consciousness (IIT, GNW, HOT, PP, AST) can generate this prediction — it is unique to the redirectable-ESM mechanism.

A second prediction may also interest you: psychedelics at sub-ego-dissolution doses should alleviate anosognosia (unawareness of deficit after stroke), because psychedelics globally increase implicit-explicit permeability, which should compensate for the local permeability block that causes anosognosia. This connects psychedelic pharmacology to clinical neurology through a single mechanism — a cross-domain prediction no other theory makes.

The full paper — including seven additional predictions — is available as a preprint (DOI: https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.18669891) and as a PDF attachment. All materials including an adversarial challenge process: https://github.com/JeltzProstetnic/aIware. The theory's criticality requirement, derived from Wolfram's computational framework in 2015, has since been independently confirmed by large-scale empirical work (Hengen & Shew, 2025; Algom & Shriki, 2026).
