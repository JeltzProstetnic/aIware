## II. CORE ARCHITECTURE (FMT)

### 5. The Four-Model Theory
The central claim: consciousness is constituted by ongoing self-simulation across four nested models arranged along two axes (scope and mode). Self-simulation as mechanism, not output. The four models as a principled minimum (the floor, not the ceiling) in a continuous modeling space.
- Source: FMT (Sections 3.1-3.2)
- Links to: The Four Models, Real/Virtual Split, Two Axes, Self-Referential Closure, Core Definition of Consciousness

### 6. Core Definition of Consciousness
Consciousness as the ability to create a model of oneself, relate that model to oneself, and interact with it — a process-based, substrate-independent, functional definition centered on mechanism rather than output.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.1)
- Links to: Four-Model Theory, Process Physicalism, Substrate Independence, Self-Referential Closure

### 7. The Two Axes: Scope and Mode
The orthogonal dimensions that generate the four models: scope (world vs. self) and mode (implicit/learned vs. explicit/generated). Why exactly these two axes, and why the 2x2 taxonomy identifies the minimum sufficient set for consciousness.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.2)
- Links to: The Four Models, Implicit World Model, Implicit Self Model, Explicit World Model, Explicit Self Model

### 8. The Four Models
Detailed treatment of each model in the architecture: Implicit World Model (IWM), Implicit Self Model (ISM), Explicit World Model (EWM), and Explicit Self Model (ESM) — their contents, properties, and functional roles.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.2)
- Links to: Implicit World Model, Implicit Self Model, Explicit World Model, Explicit Self Model, Real/Virtual Split

### 9. Implicit World Model (IWM)
The substrate's total accumulated knowledge about the world, stored in synaptic weights. Encompasses perceptual regularities, causal models, spatial relationships, semantic knowledge, motor programs. Never directly conscious. "Lights off."
- Source: FMT (Section 3.2)
- Links to: Implicit Self Model, Real/Virtual Split, Implicit-Explicit Boundary, Knowledge (RIM)

### 10. Implicit Self Model (ISM)
The substrate's accumulated self-knowledge: body schema, proprioceptive calibration, motor skills, habits, personality traits, autobiographical memory structures. Never directly conscious. No inner homunculus.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.2)
- Links to: Implicit World Model, Explicit Self Model, Meta-Problem, Anosognosia

### 11. Explicit World Model (EWM)
The conscious world — the brain's dynamic construction of a unified scene from sensory and stored data. Perceptual experience. Virtual, transient, generated from IWM and current sensory input.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.2)
- Links to: Explicit Self Model, Virtual Qualia, Unity and Binding, Psychedelic Phenomenology

### 12. Explicit Self Model (ESM)
The conscious self — the brain's continuous generation of a unified self-narrative. The sense of being a subject, having a perspective, occupying a body. Virtual, transient, redirectable. Central to ego dissolution, DID, and the Meta-Problem.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.2)
- Links to: Explicit World Model, Self-Referential Closure, Ego Dissolution, DID, Redirectable ESM, Graduated Consciousness

### 13. The Real/Virtual Split
The foundational ontological division: real side (IWM + ISM, physical, structural, learned, non-conscious, "lights off") versus virtual side (EWM + ESM, generated, transient, phenomenal, "lights on"). Software-like properties of the virtual side: forkable, cloneable, redirectable, reconfigurable.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.3)
- Links to: Virtual Qualia, Hard Problem Dissolution, Two-Level Ontology, Process Physicalism

### 14. Self-Referential Closure
Why self-referential computation has experience when a weather simulation does not. The ESM models the system modeling itself, collapsing the inside/outside distinction. Experience as observation-from-inside-the-loop, not an addition to the process.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.4)
- Links to: Virtual Qualia, Hard Problem Dissolution, Graduated Consciousness, Core Definition

## III. DISSOLVING THE HARD PROBLEM

### 15. Virtual Qualia
The theory's central claim: qualia are constitutive properties of the computational level — digital constructs that exist at the level of the running computation but are incoherent at the substrate level, like a spreadsheet cell's value is incoherent at the transistor level.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.4)
- Links to: Hard Problem Dissolution, Two-Level Ontology, Self-Referential Closure, Real/Virtual Split

### 16. Hard Problem Dissolution
The Hard Problem rests on a category error — a level confusion. The substrate does not "feel like" anything; qualia exist at the computational level where they are constitutive, not mysterious. Why this is not illusionism (Dennett, Frankish) and not deflationary (Graziano).
- Source: FMT (Section 3.4)
- Links to: Virtual Qualia, Explanatory Gap, Two-Level Ontology, Category Error

### 17. The Category Error (Level Confusion)
Precise articulation of the level confusion in the Hard Problem's formulation. Asking why neuronal firing feels like something is analogous to asking why transistor switching "is" a spreadsheet. The neurons are not the experience; they generate and sustain the computation in which experience is constitutive.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.4)
- Links to: Hard Problem Dissolution, Five-System Hierarchy, Virtual Qualia

### 18. The Explanatory Gap
How the Explanatory Gap (Levine, 1983) closes simultaneously with the Hard Problem. The gap between "neurons fire in pattern X" and "I experience red" is not a gap in knowledge but a reflection of the level distinction.
- Source: FMT (Sections 2.2, 3.4)
- Links to: Hard Problem Dissolution, Category Error, Virtual Qualia

### 19. Two-Level Ontology
The substrate level (real side) has no experience; the computational level (virtual side) has genuine experience. Both levels are physical. This is not dualism — it is a level distinction within a single physical system. Process physicalism.
- Source: FMT (Sections 3.4, 4.1)
- Links to: Real/Virtual Split, Process Physicalism, Virtual Qualia, Five-System Hierarchy

### 20. The Meta-Problem Dissolved
Why consciousness *seems* mysterious: the ESM cannot directly observe the ISM's mechanisms. The self-model is mostly sealed off from its own generative machinery, with occasional leaks. The mystery of consciousness is a *prediction* of the theory, not evidence against it.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.8)
- Links to: Explicit Self Model, Implicit Self Model, Implicit-Explicit Boundary, Hard Problem Dissolution

## IV. PHYSICAL FOUNDATIONS

### 21. The Criticality Requirement
Consciousness requires the substrate to operate at or near the edge of chaos — Wolfram's Class 4 computational regime. Derived from Wolfram's computational universality framework (2002) in Gruber (2015), independently confirmed by the empirical criticality program (Beggs & Plenz 2003, Hengen & Shew 2025, ConCrit framework).
- Source: FMT (Section 3.7)
- Links to: Wolfram's Four Classes, Cortical Automaton, Two Thresholds, Criticality Evidence

### 22. Wolfram's Four Classes
The classification of computational systems: Class 1 (fixed state), Class 2 (periodic), Class 3 (chaotic), Class 4 (edge of chaos, capable of universal computation). Only Class 4 supports consciousness.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.7)
- Links to: Criticality Requirement, Cortical Automaton, Edge of Chaos

### 23. The Cortical Automaton
The instantaneous pattern of neural firing across the cortex as a literal cellular automaton: cortical columns as cells, six-layer architecture and lateral connectivity as transition rules, operating in a many-thousand-dimensional space. Not consciousness itself, but the computational medium.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.7.2)
- Links to: Criticality Requirement, Five-System Hierarchy, Phosphenes and Visual Snow

### 24. The Five-System Hierarchy
Five hierarchically nested systems in the brain: (1) Physical, (2) Electrochemical, (3) Proteomic, (4) Topological (where implicit models are stored), (5) Virtual (where consciousness exists). Each level fully physical and fully determined by the level below. Seeking experiential properties at Levels 1-4 is the category error.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.7.1)
- Links to: Category Error, Two-Level Ontology, Criticality Requirement, Real/Virtual Split

### 25. Two Thresholds for Consciousness
The computational threshold (criticality) and the architectural threshold (four-model architecture). Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone. Together they are sufficient.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.7.3)
- Links to: Criticality Requirement, Four-Model Theory, Boundary Problem

### 26. Criticality Evidence (Independent Convergence)
Timeline of convergence between the theoretical prediction (Gruber 2015 via Wolfram) and empirical findings: neuronal avalanches (Beggs & Plenz 2003), Entropic Brain Hypothesis (Carhart-Harris 2014), LSD and criticality (Tagliazucchi 2016), meta-analysis of 140 datasets (Hengen & Shew 2025), ConCrit framework (Algom & Shriki 2026).

## V. KEY MECHANISMS

### 27. The Implicit-Explicit Boundary
The selectively permeable boundary between implicit models (substrate-level) and explicit models (phenomenal). Information becomes conscious when transferred from implicit to explicit. Variable permeability explains psychedelics, anosognosia, pre-sleep imagery, meditation.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.6)
- Links to: Psychedelic Phenomenology, Anosognosia, Sleep States, Graded Permeability

### 28. Variable Permeability
The central explanatory mechanism: permeability of the implicit-explicit boundary is not fixed but dynamically variable. Global increases (psychedelics), local decreases (anosognosia), gradual changes (pre-sleep), trained modulation (meditation). Even normal states show subtle leaks (blind-spot filling, phosphenes, visual snow).
- Source: FMT (Sections 3.6, 6.1-6.6)
- Links to: Implicit-Explicit Boundary, Psychedelic Phenomenology, Anosognosia, Prediction 1

### 29. Graduated Levels of Consciousness
Consciousness is not binary but graduated: basic consciousness (minimal self-simulation), simply extended (first-order self-observation), doubly extended (metacognition), triply extended (philosophical reflection, the ability to study consciousness itself). Organisms fluctuate between levels.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.5)
- Links to: Explicit Self Model, Animal Consciousness, Recursive Self-Modeling, Meta-Problem

### 30. The Redirectable ESM
The ESM requires input. Disrupt normal self-referential input and it latches onto whatever input dominates. Key mechanism behind ego dissolution phenomenology and salvia divinorum "becoming" experiences. Basis of Prediction 2.
- Source: FMT (Sections 3.3, 6.1, 8.3)
- Links to: Explicit Self Model, Ego Dissolution, Prediction 2, Psychedelic Phenomenology

### 31. Virtual Model Forking
The virtual models can be forked: a single substrate can run multiple configurations of the ESM simultaneously. Key mechanism behind dissociative identity disorder (multiple alters as distinct ESM configurations on the same substrate).
- Source: FMT (Sections 3.3, 6.2)
- Links to: Real/Virtual Split, DID, Prediction 3

### 32. Holographic Storage
The implicit models store information in a distributed (holographic) manner: each part contains a degraded version of the whole. Explains split-brain bilateral degradation (Pinto et al. 2017) and graceful degradation under brain damage. The holography-criticality nexus as an open research question.
- Source: FMT (Sections 5.2, 6.4, 9)
- Links to: Split-Brain Phenomena, Criticality Requirement, Open Questions

### 33. The Dual Evaluation Architecture
The relationship between substrate and simulation is not epiphenomenal: the implicit system deploys the virtual simulation as its evaluation mechanism. The explicit models assess outcomes, run scenarios, register hedonic valence. Two-way traffic: implicit uses explicit for evaluation; explicit evaluations reshape implicit models through learning.
- Source: FMT (Section 4.2)
- Links to: Causal Role of Consciousness, Process Physicalism, Consciousness as Process

## VI. PHILOSOPHICAL COMMITMENTS

### 34. Process Physicalism
The theory's physicalist position: both substrate and simulation are physical processes. No non-physical substance. Qualia are higher-order physical patterns. Process physicalism (not identity theory, not traditional functionalism) — consciousness is constituted by the *process* of self-simulation, not identical to any particular neural state.
- Source: FMT (Section 4.1)
- Links to: Two-Level Ontology, Substrate Independence, Virtual Qualia

### 35. Consciousness as Process, Not Agent
Consciousness is not an entity that causes things — it is a process performed by the substrate. Asking whether consciousness "causes" anything is a category error (the clock pointer analogy). The dual evaluation architecture explains the functional role without independent causal power.
- Source: FMT (Section 4.2)
- Links to: Dual Evaluation Architecture, Causal Role of Consciousness, Epiphenomenalism

### 36. Substrate Independence
Consciousness depends on function (four models at criticality), not on material (biological neurons). The six-layer mammalian cortex is an evolutionary implementation, not a requirement. Implications for artificial consciousness.
- Source: FMT (Sections 3.1, 4.4, 8.6)
- Links to: Artificial Consciousness, Two Thresholds, Four-Model Theory

### 37. Weak Emergence
Consciousness emerges weakly from the substrate — no strong emergence, no ontological gap between levels. This avoids the Combination Problem (panpsychism) and the mysteriousness of strong emergence. Each level of the five-system hierarchy is fully determined by the level below.
- Source: FMT (Sections 4.1, 2.6)
- Links to: Five-System Hierarchy, Combination and Emergence, Process Physicalism

### 38. Not Illusionism, Not Deflationary
How the theory differs from Dennett's illusionism and Frankish's qualia eliminativism: qualia are *real at the computational level*, not illusions. Also not compatible with Graziano's deflationary account. What is illusory is the assumption that phenomenal character must be a property of the physical substrate.
- Source: FMT (Section 3.4)
- Links to: Virtual Qualia, Hard Problem Dissolution, IIT Comparison, AST Comparison

## X. THE RECURSIVE INTELLIGENCE MODEL (RIM)

### 63. The Recursive Intelligence Model (Overview)
Intelligence as a recursive, self-reinforcing system of three components (Knowledge, Performance, Motivation) rather than a static trait. The systematic exclusion of motivation from intelligence models as a structural blind spot.
- Source: RIM
- Links to: Three Components, Recursive Loop, Consciousness-Intelligence Bridge, Operational Knowledge

### 64. The Three Components: Knowledge, Performance, Motivation
Detailed treatment of each component: Knowledge (*Wissen* — factual + operational), Performance (*Leistung* — processing capacity, working memory, speed), Motivation (Wissensdrang + Handlungsdrang). Their correspondence to existing constructs (Gc, Gf, need for cognition).
- Source: RIM (Section 3.1)
- Links to: Recursive Loop, Operational Knowledge, Cattell's Investment Theory

### 65. The Recursive Loop
The closed amplification loop: Knowledge enhances Performance (learning strategies improve processing), Performance enhances Knowledge (greater capacity enables deeper learning), Motivation enhances both (sustained engagement), and success enhances Motivation (self-efficacy, Matthew effect). Why this is not simply "motivation matters."
- Source: RIM (Sections 3.2-3.3)
- Links to: Three Components, Matthew Effect, Compounding Effects, Operational Knowledge

### 66. Operational Knowledge (Metawissen): The Hidden Multiplier
The distinction between factual knowledge (content) and operational knowledge (how to learn and think — learning strategies, reasoning heuristics, metacognitive skills). Operational knowledge is multiplicative, not additive: it amplifies the *rate* of all subsequent learning.
- Source: RIM (Section 4)
- Links to: Three Components, Recursive Loop, Educational Implications, AI Implications

### 67. Wissensdrang and Handlungsdrang
The two sub-components of Motivation: Wissensdrang (thirst for knowledge, intrinsic drive to understand) and Handlungsdrang (urge to act, drive to apply knowledge and experiment). Their alignment with Self-Determination Theory and need for cognition.
- Source: RIM (Section 3.1)
- Links to: Three Components, Motivation, Self-Determination Theory

### 68. The Matthew Effect and Compounding Dynamics
How the recursive loop produces self-reinforcing dynamics: small initial differences compound over time, producing wide variance in adult intellectual achievement. Why the Matthew effect in reading (Stanovich 1986) is a specific instance of the general recursive loop.
- Source: RIM (Sections 3.2, 6.4)
- Links to: Recursive Loop, Compounding Effects, Educational Implications

### 69. Gf-Gc Divergence Across the Lifespan
The recursive model explains why fluid intelligence (Gf) declines from early adulthood while crystallized intelligence (Gc) continues to grow: the growing dominance of Knowledge and Motivation as the Performance component (biological processing capacity) declines.
- Source: RIM (Section 3.2)
- Links to: Three Components, Cattell's Investment Theory, Performance Is Not the Bottleneck

### 70. The Flynn Effect and Its Reversal
Population-level IQ changes explained by the recursive model: environmental conditions supporting the loop (education, engagement) strengthen it; degradation of those conditions weakens it. The "Austrian paradox" (IQ up, *g* down) as teaching-to-the-test inflating Performance scores without engaging the recursive loop.
- Source: RIM (Section 3.3)
- Links to: Recursive Loop, Compounding Effects, Operational Knowledge

### 71. Performance Is Not the Bottleneck
For the vast majority of people, cognitive processing capacity is not the binding constraint on intellectual development. Average working memory capacity is sufficient for highly intelligent real-world behavior. The binding constraints are Knowledge and Motivation.
- Source: RIM (Section 6.2)
- Links to: Three Components, Recursive Loop, Educational Implications

### 72. Relation to Established Intelligence Models
How RIM relates to and extends CHC taxonomy, Cattell's investment theory, Sternberg's triarchic/adaptive/meta-intelligence, Ackerman's PPIK theory, Duckworth's grit, Stanovich's Rationality Quotient, Snow's cognitive-conative-affective framework, and dynamic systems approaches (Van Geert, Balboni).
- Source: RIM (Sections 3.4, 7.1)
- Links to: Three Components, Recursive Loop, Historical Context
